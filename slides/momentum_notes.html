In this video, we introduce momentum balances, which are also sometimes referred to as force balances. The basic idea of performing momentum balances is exactly the same as in the case of performing mass, species, or energy balances.

One big difference, however, is that momentum is a vector quantity, while mass, the number of a particular species, and energy are all scalar quantities.

Recall that momentum is the product of the mass of a system and its velocity, which is a vector. This means that momentum not only has a magnitude, but it also a direction. We can deal with this by considering each component of the momentum separately. For example, the balance for the $x$-, $y$-, and $z$-components of the momentum can each be considered separately and independently. The main subtlety that still remains, however, is that increase or decrease of momentum depends on what direction is chose as the "positive" direction. That is, an increase in the value of the component of the momentum means a higher velocity in the positive direction.

This complication is not too difficult, however, as long as we keep it in mind when we are performing our balances.

The next element of momentum balances is linking forces to the generation of momentum. This is given by Newton's second law of motion.

In this problem, we only consider the $x$-component of the momentum, which is equal to the mass of the system multiplied by the $x$-component of its velocity. Our coordinate system is such that we consider motion to the right to be motion in the positive $x$-direction. Positive values of momentum correspond to motion to the right, while negative values of the momentum correspond to motion to the left.
