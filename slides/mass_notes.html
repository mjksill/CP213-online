Balance equation for mass

\begin{align*} {\rm accumulation} &= {\rm inflow} - {\rm outflow} + {\rm generation} \end{align*}
  • accumulation: \(\Delta m\), \(\Delta(\rho V)\), etc.
  • flow terms:
    • \(\dot{m}\Delta t\)
    • \(\rho \dot{V}\Delta t\) or \(\rho v A\Delta t\)
  • generation

Tank filling

Consider filling a tank with water at an unknown volumetric flow rate \(\dot{V}(t)\) from a tap, which could vary with time. How can we determine the flow rate?

A simple way to do this is to get a container and a stop watch. We let the container fill with water for a certain period of time \(\Delta t\) (e.g., 30\,seconds). We then measure the volume of liquid in the container after that period of time.

We typically don't directly measure the volume of the liquid in the container. Most of the time, we simply measure the height \(h\) of the liquid level. If the inner cross sectional area \(A\) of the container is constant with respect to the height (e.g., the container is a cylinder), then the volume of water in the container is related to the height \(h\) of the liquid as

\begin{align*} V &= A h . \end{align*}

Determining the water level

Now let's think of the problem in the reverse direction. We now have a flow meter that gives us the volumetric flow rate \(\dot{V}(t)\) of water with time. Now we ask the question "How does the water level in the tank vary with time?".

If we assume that the volumetric flow rate is constant, then we have

\begin{align*} A[h(t)-h(0)] &= + \dot{V} t \\ h(t) &= h(0) + \frac{\dot{V}}{A} t \end{align*}

More generally, we can write how the height of the water level changes between a time interval \(\Delta t\) as

\begin{align*} h(t+\delta t) &= h(t) + \frac{\dot{V}}{A}\Delta t \end{align*}
