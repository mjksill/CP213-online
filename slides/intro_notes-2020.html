Key learning objectives

  • Problem solving
  • Mathematics
  • Programming with Python

The learning objectives for this module can be divided into three main areas.

The first area is problem solving, which in this module will mainly involve formulating mathematical descriptions of different engineering problems.

The biggest challenge in mathematics is typically not the manipulation of mathematical expressions or following methods for solving equations. Rather it is understanding how to relate the mathematics to real world problems. Typically, the math problems that we find most difficult in school are "word problems". This because it is hard to recognize the connection between elements of the abstract mathematics and their concrete incarnations.

The other area we will be addressing is mathematics. We will be developing our ability to analyze and solve the mathematical models that we develop. Part of this will involve revising and reinforcing your prior knowledge of mathematics, but we will also cover some new material, including analytical and numerical methods for solving equations.

Finally, we will be introducing you to programming, using Python. At the most basic level, it provides a more efficient way for you to do computations that you would normally perform using your calculator; however, it allows you easily repeat your calculations, as well as provides a record of exactly how they were done. However, programming is much more powerful that this, and we will explore how it can be used to provide solutions to mathematical problems that cannot be solved analytically.

    • The planned schedule for semester 1 is shown here. It is formally divided into four main sections.

    In the first week, the programming language Python will be introduced. Don't worry, as there is no expectation for you to have any prior programming experience. There will be no need to install any software on your computer to do the programming exercises. Everything will be done through Jupyter notebooks, which will be accessible through your web browser.

    The next three weeks will devoted learning about differential balances, which is essentially applying the concepts of mass and energy balances that you were introduced to last year to systems that are non-uniform or with properties that may vary in time.

    These differential balances will result in equations known as differential equations, which relate variables, as well as their derivatives to each other. In weeks 5-8, we will learn methods for solving these types of equations.

    The last topic we will touch on is multivariate calculus, where we will examine functions that depend on more than one independent variable. This will allow us to examine problems such as quantifying the impact of uncertainty on our calculations, fitting model parameters to experimental data, and the diffusion of mass or heat through a material.
